Week 3: Design Thinking Process

Date: 7th Oct 2025 - 13th Oct 2025 | Human-Centered Problem Solving

Process Overview

  • Focus: Solving navigation safety issues for Nile boat operators
  • Methodology: 5-stage Design Thinking process
  • Outcome: "Port of Safety" checkpoint concept
  • Key Insight: Economic pressures and environmental constraints create dangerous conditions

The Problem Space

1 Identify the Challenge

The system of overloaded, long-distance passenger boats traveling on the Nile at night presents significant safety risks for operators and passengers alike.

Safety Navigation Overloading Night Travel

2 Empathize with Users

Through synthesized interviews and observations, we developed a deep understanding of the boat operators' experiences and challenges.

"A boat operator. The trip is 48 hours. I must travel at night. If I don't carry extra people and goods, I don't make a profit. But when I use my bright white light to see, it blinds me and others because it reflects off the water. We are all navigating blind."

Empathy Map for a Typical Boat Operator:

Says
  • "The light just makes a white wall."
  • "I need to carry more to earn more."
  • "We navigate by memory and sound."
Thinks
  • "This is dangerous, but I have no choice."
  • "I cannot see other boats until we are too close."
  • "The system forces me to take risks."
Feels
  • Trapped by economic necessity
  • Constant stress and anxiety
  • Fearful of causing an accident
Does
  • Turns lights off to reduce glare
  • Overloads the boat for profitability
  • Navigates by memory and sound

3 Define the Problem

Based on our empathy work, we formulated a clear problem statement:

Problem Statement: Boat operators and passengers need a way to travel safely on the 48-hour journey without the risk of collision or sinking because boats are overloaded to be profitable and traditional white lights create a dangerous glare on the water, hiding other vessels.

The Solution Sprint

4 Ideate Solutions

Using brainstorming techniques, we generated a wide range of potential solutions to address the identified problems.

Amber & Red Lights

Use non-glare-colored lights for night visibility that don't reflect off water.

Painted Load Line

A mandatory waterline mark on every boat to indicate maximum capacity.

Port Weigh Station

A scale to check boat weight before departure to prevent overloading.

Underwater Acoustic Pingers

Devices that make boats "hear" each other in low visibility conditions.

Radar Reflectors

Passive devices to make boats show up on radar systems.

Community Safety Certification

A flag system for boats that follow safety protocols.

5 Concept Development

We selected the three most promising ideas and developed them into detailed concepts with annotated sketches.

Concept 1: The "Night-Vision" Boat Kit

"This integrated solution directly prevents overloading and enables safe night vision without glare."

Night-Vision Boat Kit Concept Sketch

Key Features:
  • Amber-colored navigation lights to reduce glare
  • Integrated load monitoring system
  • Solar-powered for sustainability
  • Water-resistant design for marine environment

Concept 2: The "Port of Safety" Checkpoint

"Procedural solution: 1. Verify weight 2. Inspect lights 3. Certify departure."

Verification Steps:
  • Weight verification at digital scale
  • Light inspection for proper amber/red lighting
  • Safety certification issuance

Concept 3: The "Acoustic Awareness" System

"Non-visual backup system. Works in all weather and light conditions."

System Components:
  • Underwater acoustic transmitters
  • Directional audio receivers
  • Proximity alert system

6 Selected Concept

After evaluating all concepts against feasibility, desirability, and viability criteria, we selected:

Concept 2: The "Port of Safety" Checkpoint

Justification: This is the most viable and feasible solution because it creates a single, enforceable point of control that systematically prevents overloading and mandates the use of non-glare lights, directly addressing both root causes of the problem.

Process Reflection

This task was a powerful demonstration of how a structured design thinking process can untangle a complex, real-world problem and generate human-centered, practical solutions.

The process revealed that what initially appeared as simple "annoying boats" was actually a complex system of economic pressures (overloading) and environmental constraints (glare) that create dangerous conditions.

In conclusion, the ultimate value of this process was its ability to reframe the problem from a technical issue to a systemic one. The final proposed solution—a mandatory safety certification combining non-glare lights and load checks—is compelling not because it's high-tech, but because it is feasible, desirable, and directly addresses the nuanced root causes we uncovered.

This approach demonstrates how design thinking moves beyond assumptions to create solutions that are both intelligent and deeply empathetic to the people they serve.
